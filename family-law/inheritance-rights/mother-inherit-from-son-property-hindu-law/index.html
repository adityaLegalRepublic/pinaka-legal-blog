Mother’s Right to Inherit From Her Son’s Property

Imagine this: Your son passed away, and within days, relatives start saying that everything belongs to his wife and children, and that you — his own mother — have no claim. Some people say the property automatically goes to the widow. Others say you can’t inherit because you weren’t dependent on him. You are grieving, you are uncertain, and you are afraid of losing what your son may have intended for you.

This kind of confusion is very common. But it is also wrong. Under the Hindu Succession Act, 1956 (called “HSA” throughout this article), the law is clear: a mother is a Class I heir of her deceased son. She does not need to prove dependency, wait for the wife and children to inherit first, or get anyone’s permission. Her right is immediate, statutory, and absolute.

This article explains exactly what that means for you — in plain language, without the textbook jargon.

What Does the Law Actually Say?

When a Hindu man dies without leaving a will (this is called “dying intestate”), his property passes according to the rules in the HSA. The starting point is Section 8 of the Hindu Succession Act, 1956, which divides heirs into four categories:

  1. Class I heirs (from the Schedule to the Act)
  2. Class II heirs (from the Schedule)
  3. Agnates
  4. Cognates

The law says that if there are any Class I heirs alive at the time of the son’s death, they inherit first and everyone else is excluded. Class II heirs, agnates, and cognates get nothing as long as even one Class I heir is alive.

And here is the critical part for every mother reading this: the Schedule to the HSA explicitly lists “mother” as a Class I heir. Not Class II. Not a secondary heir. Class I — the primary tier.

Mother as a Class I Heir

The commentaries on the HSA explain this clearly. The Act lists 16 Class I heirs (there were 12 before the Amendment Act of 2005 added four more). Among these 16, the four primary heirs are: the son, the daughter, the widow, and the mother.

The Act deliberately places the mother in Class I while placing the father in Class II. This was a conscious legislative choice. The commentary on the Act notes that Vijnaneshwara, in the Mitakshara tradition, held that the mother’s “propinquity is greatest” — that is, no one is closer to a child than the mother. The HSA adopted this view. The result is that:

If a man dies possessed of property leaving behind only his mother and father and no other heir in Class I of the Schedule, the mother would take the whole estate of the son to the entire exclusion of the father.

This is not a loophole or a technicality. It is the plain operation of the statute. The mother outranks the father in Hindu succession law.

The law on the mother’s identity is also very broad. The commentary makes clear that a mother is always a mother:

  • Whether the son was born of a valid marriage, a void marriage, or a voidable marriage
  • Whether the mother is a natural mother or an adoptive mother
  • Even if the son was illegitimate

A step-mother, however, is not included in the term “mother” for purposes of Class I — she may inherit as a Class II heir as the “father’s widow.”

What Share Does the Mother Get?

Section 10 of the HSA lays down the rules for distributing property among Class I heirs. This is where Rules 1 and 2 become important for a mother.

Rules 1 and 2 state: the mother, widow, surviving sons and daughters each take one share. There is no preference among them. All of them are equal at the table.

The law commentaries give concrete worked examples:

  • If the mother and widow are the only survivors: The mother gets 1/2 and the widow gets 1/2. They split the property equally.
  • If the mother, widow, one son, and one daughter all survive: Each takes 1/4. The mother’s share is exactly equal to the widow’s, the son’s, and the daughter’s.
  • If the mother is the only survivor (no widow, no children): The mother takes the entire property. The father, being a Class II heir, is excluded entirely.

This is called per capita distribution — meaning each heir of the same class gets an equal, individual share. There is no distinction based on gender or the side of the family from which the heir comes.

The mother’s share is not a limited or life interest. Her right in the property is absolute. She can sell it, gift it, or pass it to her own heirs. It becomes her property completely.

What If the Son Had a Wife and Children?

This is the question most mothers ask first, and with good reason. The son’s wife (widow) and the son’s children are also Class I heirs. Does their presence push the mother out?

The answer is: No.

Section 9 of the HSA says that all Class I heirs inherit simultaneously. The widow does not take first. The children do not take first. Everyone in Class I stands at the same level and shares the property at the same time.

So if your son left behind: his widow (W), two daughters (D1, D2), and you (his mother, M) — all four of you would get equal shares of 1/4 each. You are not pushed behind or waiting in line.

What about a situation where the son left a will? If your son left a valid will, the will governs. But if the will does not mention you or if your son died without a will, the HSA kicks in automatically and protects your share.

The case of Savithri Amma v. Devaki Amma is worth knowing about. In that case, a son died leaving behind his widow and children. The question arose whether his mother could still claim as a Class I heir. The court held that the mother’s claim was valid and was not barred — her right under the HSA stood firm.

Does It Matter If the Mother Remarried?

Many mothers worry about this, especially if they remarried after the son’s father died or if there were difficult family circumstances. The law is clear here too.

The HSA commentary states: her remarriage or her unchastity is not a bar. The two cases often cited on this point are Mantorabai v. Paretanbai, AIR 1972 MP 145 and Jayalakshmi Ammal v. Ganesa Iyer, AIR 1972 Mad 357. Both held that unchastity or remarriage of the mother is no ground for her disinheritance under the HSA.

This reflects the Act’s core principle: the mother’s right flows from the relationship of motherhood, not from her conduct or marital status at any point in her life.

What About the Father — Does He Also Inherit?

This surprises many families. The father of a deceased Hindu man is placed in Class II of the Schedule — not Class I. He is the first heir in Class II.

This means: as long as any Class I heir is alive, the father gets nothing. If the mother, widow, and children are all alive, the father is completely excluded from the inheritance.

If only the mother and father survive the son (no widow, no children, no grandchildren), then the mother takes the entire property as the sole Class I heir. The father gets nothing.

The commentary notes that this was seen as an anomaly by some scholars — arguing that both parents should be in Class I since both have maintenance obligations towards the child. The Law Commission has recommended including fathers in Class I, but as of now, the law remains as enacted: mother in Class I, father in Class II.

This means that if you are a mother and someone in the family says “your husband (the son’s father) will inherit, not you,” that is legally incorrect under the current HSA.

What Should I Actually Do Now?

If your son has passed away and you want to assert your inheritance rights, here are the practical steps you should follow:

  1. Collect documents proving your relationship: Your birth certificate, the son’s birth certificate, your marriage certificate if available — anything that establishes you are the mother of the deceased.
  2. Identify all the property your son owned: This includes land, house, bank accounts, fixed deposits, shares, provident fund, vehicles. Make a list with whatever documents you can find (sale deeds, bank passbooks, etc.).
  3. Check whether your son left a will: If there is a registered will, it governs the distribution. If not, the HSA applies and your Class I status is automatic.
  4. Find out who the other Class I heirs are: The widow and children are the most likely co-heirs. You are all equal — no one can exclude you.
  5. For immovable property (land/house): Apply for mutation of the property records at the local tehsil/revenue office or municipality. All Class I heirs must typically apply together, or you can apply individually and request your share.
  6. For bank accounts and fixed deposits: Each bank has a process for handling accounts of deceased persons. You will need a death certificate, proof of relationship, and in some cases, a succession certificate from a civil court.
  7. If the family is refusing to share: You can file a civil suit for partition and separate possession of your share. A lawyer can help you file this in the appropriate civil court.
  8. Do not sign away your rights: Family members may pressure you to sign “No Objection Certificates” or relinquishment deeds. Do not sign anything without consulting a lawyer first.
  9. Approach a lawyer early: Delays in asserting inheritance claims can create practical complications (property may be sold, transferred, or disputed further). The earlier you act, the better.

If you are dealing with property disputes within the family, understanding how wills and succession work under Hindu law can also help you see the full picture of your rights.

You Have a Right the Law Recognises

Losing a son is a grief that no law can address. But the law does try to ensure that a mother is not left without her rightful share of what her son built in his lifetime.

The Hindu Succession Act places you in the highest category of heirs — Class I — alongside the widow and children. Your share is equal. Your right is immediate. Your ownership is absolute, not conditional.

No one can legally tell you that you must wait, that you must depend on the widow’s goodwill, or that the father takes before you. If you are facing those situations, the law is on your side. What you need is someone who can help you enforce it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the mother really a Class I heir under Hindu law?

Yes, absolutely. The Schedule to the Hindu Succession Act, 1956 explicitly lists the mother as a Class I heir of a deceased Hindu male. She is among the four primary heirs — along with the widow, son, and daughter. Class I heirs inherit first and to the exclusion of all other categories. This is not a matter of interpretation — it is the plain statutory text.

Can the mother’s right to inherit from her son’s property be taken away?

No, not without her consent. As a Class I heir, her right vests automatically when her son dies without a will. No family member, no court, and no other heir can unilaterally cancel her share. She can voluntarily relinquish her share (by signing a relinquishment deed), but this must be her own free decision. She should never sign such a document under pressure and should consult a lawyer first.

What share does the mother get if the son also had a wife and two children?

If the son left behind his mother, his widow, and two children (say, one son and one daughter), all four are Class I heirs. Under Section 10 of the HSA (Rules 1 and 2), each takes one share. So the property is divided into four equal parts: mother gets 1/4, widow gets 1/4, the son gets 1/4, and the daughter gets 1/4.

Does the father inherit along with the mother?

No. The father of a deceased Hindu male is a Class II heir, placed in Entry I of Class II. Class II heirs only inherit if there are no Class I heirs alive. Since the mother is a Class I heir, the father is excluded as long as the mother is alive and has not relinquished her share. This is one of the most misunderstood points in Hindu succession law.

Does it matter if the mother’s son was illegitimate?

The mother remains entitled to inherit from an illegitimate son. The commentary on the HSA makes clear that the mother is an heir to her illegitimate son. The Act’s approach is broad: motherhood — including biological motherhood of an illegitimate child — creates inheritance rights. This is consistent with the Act’s overall objective of protecting female heirs.

What if the mother remarried after the son’s father passed away? Does that disqualify her?

No. Under the HSA, remarriage of the mother is not a bar to inheritance. The cases of Mantorabai v. Paretanbai (AIR 1972 MP 145) and Jayalakshmi Ammal v. Ganesa Iyer (AIR 1972 Mad 357) confirm that unchastity or remarriage of the mother does not disinherit her. Her right flows from the fact of motherhood, not from her conduct or personal choices.

What if the son left a will giving everything to his wife?

A Hindu male has the right to dispose of his separate property by will. If the son left a valid registered will leaving everything to his wife, that will generally governs and the mother may not get a share from that particular property. However, if the son had any property that the will does not cover, or if the will is challenged as invalid, the HSA rules apply. Consult a lawyer to assess whether a will is valid and complete.

My son’s mother-in-law is claiming that I have no right to the house. Is she correct?

No. The mother-in-law of your deceased son (i.e., your son’s wife’s mother) has no claim whatsoever in your son’s property under the HSA. The only heirs are those listed in the Schedule. You as the mother are a Class I heir. Your daughter-in-law is also a Class I heir. But your daughter-in-law’s mother is neither a Class I nor a Class II heir of your son.

Do I need to go to court to claim my share?

Not necessarily. If the other Class I heirs cooperate, you can mutually agree on the division and execute a partition deed. For immovable property, mutation at the revenue office completes the record change. For bank accounts, you approach the bank with documents. If others refuse to cooperate, a civil suit for partition may be needed. A lawyer can advise on which route suits your situation.

Is the mother’s share absolute or is it only a right to use the property during her lifetime?

It is absolute. The Hindu Succession Act removed the old concept of “limited estate” for female heirs. Under Section 14 of the HSA, any property a female Hindu receives by way of inheritance becomes her absolute property — she can sell it, gift it, mortgage it, or pass it to her own heirs by will. There is no restriction on her ownership.

What happens to the mother’s share in her son’s property if she also passes away?

Once the mother inherits her share from her son’s estate, that share becomes her own absolute property. When she dies, it passes according to Section 15 of the HSA (succession rules for Hindu females). Her heirs would be her own sons and daughters first, then her husband’s heirs, and so on. The property does not automatically revert to her deceased son’s widow or children unless that is who her heirs happen to be.

Can a step-mother inherit from a stepson’s property as a Class I heir?

No. The term “mother” in Class I of the Schedule does not include a step-mother. A step-mother may inherit as a Class II heir — she falls under Entry VI of Class II as the “father’s widow.” This means she only inherits if there are no Class I heirs. If the deceased’s natural or adoptive mother is alive, the step-mother (as a Class II heir) is completely excluded.
